Local electrical project planning before the electrician arrives

A clear description of the property, the problem, and the desired result helps an electrical contractor prepare for a useful first visit. This checklist is designed for homeowners and property managers planning repairs, renovations, panel work, lighting changes, or electric vehicle charging in western Quebec.

Start with the reason for the visit

Write down what prompted the call. A breaker that trips, lights that flicker, a warm receptacle, or equipment that no longer operates should be described in plain language. Note when the issue started, how often it occurs, and whether it affects one circuit or several areas of the building. Do not remove covers or touch damaged electrical components to gather more detail.

For planned work, describe the outcome instead. Useful examples include adding a dedicated circuit, improving lighting in a work area, preparing a garage for an EV charger, or reviewing whether an existing panel can support a renovation. Photos of the panel area and the proposed work location can help with early planning, but they do not replace an on-site assessment.

Collect the property details that affect the visit

  • Property type, approximate age, and whether it is occupied during the work.
  • Location of the electrical panel and whether the path to it is clear.
  • Any previous additions, renovations, or known electrical upgrades.
  • Access constraints such as tenant scheduling, locked rooms, parking, or pets.
  • The make and model of new equipment when a dedicated circuit may be required.

Older homes can contain changes made over several decades. Avoid assuming that visible finishes reveal what is behind the walls. The electrician may need to confirm circuit routing, available capacity, grounding, and the condition of existing equipment before giving a final scope.

Plan for a renovation before walls are closed

Electrical decisions are easier while framing and routes are accessible. Prepare a room-by-room list of fixed appliances, receptacle needs, switches, lighting locations, network equipment, outdoor loads, and future additions. Marking these items on a simple floor plan helps identify conflicts and reduces last-minute changes.

Ask the contractor which decisions must be final before rough-in and which can wait until fixtures are selected. Confirm who supplies decorative fixtures, smart controls, specialty devices, and equipment disconnects. Keep product documentation available so electrical requirements can be checked against the proposed installation.

Prepare for panel or capacity questions

A panel review is not based only on the number printed on the main breaker. Existing loads, available spaces, service equipment, planned additions, and the installation method all matter. List major electric equipment such as heating, hot-water systems, ranges, dryers, pools, spas, workshops, and vehicle charging. Include future plans even if they are not part of the immediate project.

If there is sparking, smoke, a burning smell, visible heat damage, or another immediate danger, keep people away from the area and contact emergency services or the appropriate utility as required. Do not wait for a routine quotation appointment.

Questions to settle before accepting a scope

  • What work is included, and what conditions could change the price?
  • Are permits, inspections, utility coordination, or service interruptions expected?
  • Who will repair drywall, paint, masonry, landscaping, or other finishes afterward?
  • Which materials and fixtures are supplied by the contractor or the customer?
  • What access, shutdown time, and scheduling coordination will be required?

A good request does not need technical vocabulary. Accurate observations, clear priorities, and realistic access information are more useful than guessing at the cause of a problem. The electrician can then confirm the safe and appropriate approach after evaluating the site.
