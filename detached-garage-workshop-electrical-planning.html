Planning electrical work for a detached garage or workshop

A detached building may need more than a few lights and receptacles. Tools, heating, vehicle charging, outdoor equipment, distance from the main building, and future uses should be considered before routes are built or finishes are installed.

Write down how the building will be used

List current activities and realistic future ones: vehicle storage, woodworking, metalworking, garden equipment, a compressor, freezers, hobby benches, exterior lighting, powered doors, heating, or charging. Note which loads might operate at the same time and whether the building will ever be occupied for extended work periods.

Create an equipment inventory

For fixed and high-demand equipment, record the manufacturer, model, voltage, and installation instructions. Portable tools may still affect receptacle locations and circuit planning. Identify where benches, cabinets, doors, and vehicles will be positioned so outlets are not hidden or placed where cords cross traffic areas.

Think about the route early

The path between the main electrical equipment and the detached building can be a major part of the project. Property features, pavement, landscaping, drainage, frost, finished interiors, and other buried services can affect options. Do not excavate until the route and required coordination are established.

Plan useful lighting

  • General lighting that reduces shadows around vehicles and work areas.
  • Task lighting at benches, machines, and storage zones.
  • Exterior lighting at doors and travel paths.
  • Switch locations that match the usual entrances.
  • Fixtures suited to temperature, dust, moisture, and impact conditions.

Provide fixture specifications before rough-in when mounting, controls, or environmental ratings affect the installation.

Include environmental and safety needs

Garages and workshops may contain moisture, dust, stored fuel, moving equipment, and large temperature changes. Explain the intended use accurately so the electrician can select an appropriate approach. Keep electrical equipment accessible and avoid planning permanent storage in required working areas.

Allow for future additions

It can be practical to discuss a future vehicle charger, larger tool, heater, pump, or exterior project while routes are accessible. Future planning does not always mean installing every circuit now, but it can prevent finished work from blocking reasonable options.
