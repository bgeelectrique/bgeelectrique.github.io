Preparing for an electrical panel and capacity review

A panel review is most useful when it considers the whole property and the loads planned for the next several years. The breaker count alone does not determine whether a service can support a renovation or new equipment.

List present and future electrical loads

Prepare a simple inventory of major equipment: space heating, water heating, range, dryer, air conditioning, pool or spa equipment, workshop tools, pumps, accessory buildings, and vehicle charging. Add proposed loads even if they will be installed in a later phase. This helps the electrician understand the project sequence and avoid planning only for today's minimum need.

Document the reason for the review

Panel work may be prompted by a renovation, an equipment addition, limited breaker spaces, obsolete components, damage, or a recommendation from another inspection. Explain the trigger and the desired result. Do not remove the panel cover or attempt to identify internal components yourself.

Prepare safe photos and records

  • A wide photo showing the panel location and surrounding access.
  • A clear photo of the closed panel door and any visible labels.
  • Equipment model numbers for planned high-demand loads.
  • Records for previous service or panel work, if available.
  • A list of circuits or areas associated with recurring problems.

Labels may be incomplete or inaccurate, especially after multiple renovations. Treat the directory as a starting point rather than proof of how every circuit is arranged.

Consider the installation environment

Storage, shelving, moisture, finished walls, stairs, and narrow utility spaces can affect safe access and the work required. Clear a practical path to the panel before the visit. If the service equipment is in a tenant area or locked room, arrange access in advance.

Ask what the assessment includes

Confirm whether the contractor will review service capacity, panel condition, available spaces, grounding and bonding, existing major loads, proposed loads, and the physical routing for new circuits. Ask what findings could change the initial scope and whether utility coordination, permits, or planned shutdowns may be needed.

Plan the project sequence

A capacity decision can affect equipment selection and renovation timing. Share deadlines for appliance delivery, wall closing, inspections, or occupancy. If several upgrades are planned, ask whether they should be grouped or staged and which decisions must be made first.
