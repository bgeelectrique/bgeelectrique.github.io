Electrical planning for a commercial tenant fit-out

A tenant improvement combines the new business layout with the limits of an existing building. Early documentation helps the electrician, landlord, designer, equipment suppliers, and other trades coordinate before the opening schedule becomes compressed.

Confirm the parties and approval path

Identify the tenant contact, landlord or property manager, designer, general contractor, and building operations contact. Clarify who can approve changes, who controls electrical rooms, and what documents the landlord requires before work begins. Building rules may govern access, deliveries, noise, shutdowns, and work outside normal hours.

Prepare an equipment schedule

List every fixed or significant electrical load with manufacturer and model details. Depending on the business, this could include kitchen equipment, refrigeration, point-of-sale systems, displays, signage, office equipment, machinery, heating, data racks, or specialty devices. Mark equipment supplied by others and identify items whose specifications are still pending.

Coordinate the floor plan

  • Show walls, doors, counters, workstations, storage, and customer areas.
  • Mark equipment and furniture that determine outlet positions.
  • Indicate lighting zones, controls, emergency routes, and exterior signs.
  • Identify data, security, audio, and communications requirements.
  • Note accessibility, cleaning, moisture, and equipment-clearance needs.

Use one current drawing set and record revisions. Conflicting plans are a common source of rework.

Review existing conditions

Arrange access to electrical rooms, panels, ceiling spaces, and tenant service equipment. Share previous drawings when available, but expect site verification. Existing labels and abandoned wiring may not accurately describe current conditions.

Protect the business schedule

Work backward from inspections, equipment delivery, staff training, and opening. Ask which electrical decisions are needed before framing, rough-in, ceiling closure, millwork installation, and final connection. Include contingency time for hidden conditions, supplier changes, and required coordination.

Define the quotation boundaries

Confirm responsibility for permits, inspections, utility or building coordination, demolition, temporary power, patching, painting, coring, fire stopping, controls programming, and equipment commissioning. A clear division of work makes competing quotes easier to compare and reduces gaps between trades.
