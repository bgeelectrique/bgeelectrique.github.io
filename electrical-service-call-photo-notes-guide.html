Photos and notes that help an electrical service call

Clear information can help a contractor prepare tools, materials, and questions for the first visit. The goal is not to inspect the electrical system yourself; it is to document visible conditions safely and accurately.

Write the observation before taking photos

Record what happened in ordinary language. Note the room or equipment, the time, how long the condition lasted, what else was operating, and whether it has happened before. For intermittent issues, a short date-and-time log may reveal a useful pattern.

Take wide and close views from a safe position

A wide photo establishes location and access. A closer photo can show a label, visible mark, fixture, or piece of equipment. Keep the image steady and well lit. Do not move covers, reach behind equipment, stand in water, use a ladder near electrical hazards, or touch anything that appears damaged.

Useful subjects to document

  • The work area and surrounding walls, ceilings, or exterior surfaces.
  • The electrical panel location with its door and cover left in place.
  • The proposed route between the panel and new equipment.
  • Manufacturer and model labels on appliances or chargers.
  • Existing switches, receptacles, or fixtures involved in the request.
  • Access restrictions such as locked rooms, tenant schedules, or parking.

Protect private information

Review images before sending them. Remove photographs that expose personal documents, alarm details, access codes, children's information, or unrelated valuables. Share only what is relevant to the requested electrical work.

Keep filenames and notes simple

Descriptive names such as “garage-charger-wall,” “panel-location-wide,” or “range-model-label” are easier to understand than a series of camera numbers. If several rooms are involved, group images by room and match each group to the written scope.

Include project decisions and deadlines

For planned work, note which equipment has been purchased, which finishes are complete, and which dates are tied to deliveries, inspections, tenants, or other trades. Mention whether walls are open and when they are expected to close. This context helps distinguish urgent coordination from optional future work.

Know when not to document

Do not approach smoke, active sparking, exposed energized components, damaged service equipment, or standing water near electrical equipment to obtain a photo. Move people away and contact emergency services or the appropriate utility when the situation calls for it.

Send the information to the right local page

For a Vaudreuil-Dorion property, use BGE's page for an electrician serving Vaudreuil-Dorion and describe the request before sending supporting images.

For Saint-Lazare, use the corresponding contact path for an electrician serving Saint-Lazare.

Photos can help with preparation, but they do not replace an on-site assessment when the condition, route, capacity, or safety of an installation must be confirmed.
